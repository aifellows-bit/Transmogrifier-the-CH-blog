The Duplicator Arcs: Calvin vs. Calvin

Plot Point Files · July 2026

Flip the transmogrifier box open-side-up and it becomes a duplicator — and the strip becomes, for two extended storylines, a comedy about the self. In the first arc, Calvin duplicates himself so the copy can go to school and clean his room while the original goes sledding. The plan fails in the only way it could: the duplicate is exactly Calvin, which means he also refuses to do chores, also wants to go sledding, and also has access to a duplicator. Soon there's a committee of Calvins, all shirking, all blaming each other, all indistinguishable to a mother who just sees one kid being impossible at unprecedented volume. The arc is a perfect closed loop: Calvin's scheme defeats Calvin because the whole premise of the scheme — that there's a version of him who does what he's told — is false by construction.

The sequel is stranger and better. Calvin bolts an "ethicator" onto the box and duplicates only his good side, producing a polite, industrious Calvin who does homework voluntarily and is (this is the fatal flaw) unfailingly sweet to Susie Derkins. The good duplicate is genuinely useful right up until he develops feelings and priorities of his own — a crush, a conscience, a note to Susie — at which point the original Calvin explodes with jealous rage, the duplicate rages back at being slandered as a mere copy, and the paradox detonates him: a good side can't sustain that much spite. He vanishes, leaving Calvin to explain to no one in particular that he's, ahem, back to normal.

Commentators love these arcs because they're the strip's most compact moral philosophy course. The first arc argues that you can't outsource your obligations to someone with your exact incentives. The second argues something trickier — that "good" isn't a detachable component; Calvin's good side severed from his appetite and ego isn't Calvin at all, but a stranger who quite reasonably prefers Susie's company. The box promised self-improvement through engineering, and both times the lesson came back: the self doesn't modularize. Also, tigers will watch the entire catastrophe while eating a sandwich, offering commentary. That's what friends are for.

Both storylines run in 1990 and are collected in Scientific Progress Goes "Boink" — or browse 1990 in the official GoComics archive.
