December 31, 1995: Anatomy of a Perfect Ending

Plot Point Files · July 2026

Endings are where comic strips go to embarrass themselves. They run decades past their best years, outlive their creators, get farmed out to assistants and heirs. Watterson did the opposite: after ten years, at the absolute peak of the strip's popularity, he announced he'd said what he came to say, and on the last day of 1995 he said goodbye in a single Sunday page.

The construction is worth slowing down for. It's a snow strip — of course it is; snow was always the strip's shorthand for possibility, a blank page falling on the whole world overnight. Calvin and Hobbes emerge to find everything buried and clean. There's no recap, no cameo parade, no character turning to wink at the reader — none of the finale clichés. Hobbes observes that the fresh snow makes the world look like new. Calvin agrees, boards the sled with his tiger, declares it a magical world — and the last line, "Let's go exploring!", sends them plunging down the hill and off the edge of the page. The final image is mostly white space. Watterson ends the strip with an open format and an open verb.

Reading the last panel closely, three choices do the work. The exit is downhill and accelerating — the strip ends at speed, in motion, in the middle of the kind of wagon-ride argument it staged a hundred times, not at rest. The ending is addressed to the reader as much as the tiger — "let's" is an invitation, and thirty years of tribute cartoons and tattoos suggest people took it personally. And the strip settles nothing — Hobbes boards the sled as a tiger, the question of what he is stays permanently open, and the snow erases the landscape the way the ending erases any possibility of a sequel: the world is blank again, yours now. Cartoonists still cite it as the best exit in the history of the form, and the closing line has become genuine cultural shorthand — a commencement-speech staple that, unusually for commencement-speech staples, actually earns it.

Read the final strip at GoComics — December 31, 1995, then read the first strip right after. The symmetry (a trap, a tuna sandwich, a tiger; then a sled, a snowfall, the same tiger) is its own essay.
